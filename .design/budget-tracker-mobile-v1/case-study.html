01The problem

From the user's own mouth, captured during the grill phase:

I make a budget at the start of the month and then forget about it. Two weeks in, I have no idea whether I'm on track. The plan and my real spending drift apart silently until the end of the month, when I either feel vague guilt or pretend it didn't happen.

Most budget apps fall into one of two camps:

Enthusiastic fintech

Mint · Cleo · Copilot Money

Bright colors, streaks, push spam. Reads like a friend with too much caffeine.

Power-user dashboards

YNAB · Lunch Money

Beautifully dense. Punishing if you're not already a budget nerd.

We wanted neither. A budget that lives in your pocket. Quiet, trustworthy, disciplined enough to do less.

02Process

Instead of opening Figma and pushing pixels, we worked through a six-phase design flow. Each phase produces a deliverable that feeds the next.

01

Grill Me

Pressure-test every decision branch.

02

Brief

Problem, principles, aesthetic.

03

IA

Site map, flows, naming.

04

Tokens

Colors, type, spacing, motion.

05

Tasks

Vertical-slice build order.

06

Review

Audit against the brief.

The rule: a phase can't start until the previous one has settled. No tokens until we know the aesthetic. No tasks until we know the IA. The flow makes it harder to skip the hard thinking — and easier for a future team to read why every screen looks the way it does.

03The four decisions that shaped the product

Decision 01

Local-only. No account. No backend.

Personal-finance apps die when users see a signup form before they've seen any value. The first 10 seconds had to be: open app → currency picker → setup → home screen. No account, no email, no password. The data lives on the device.

Cloud sync ships as optional in v1.0 (planned via Supabase + anonymous auth). It is never required.

Decision 02

One screen, one answer.

Most fintech home screens try to answer six questions at once. Ours answers one: today's safe-to-spend. The hero numeral is impossible to miss. Below it, four category bars answer "what's left in each bucket?" Below those, recent activity answers "did the last thing I logged go in?"

That's it. No watchlist. No insights chart. No marketing card.

Decision 03

Manual logging, not bank sync.

The hardest trade-off. Bank sync is what fintech users expect — auto-import, no manual entry. But it carries hidden costs: a backend, recurring API fees, security responsibility, and a category-mapping problem that's only ever 80% right.

We chose manual logging with friction-killing UX: tap the FAB, the amount field auto-focuses with the number pad already open, last-used category pre-selected.

Brief target: 5 seconds. We ship at ~3.

Decision 04

Apple-Wallet calm as the visual anchor.

We named references explicitly — Apple Wallet, Apple Money, Things 3 — and anti-references just as explicitly:

Anything with a streak counter, gamification, mascots, or a chart with more than two colors is out.

04Inside the system

A few execution choices that mattered.

🎨

Tokens ship in code, not Figma

Every spacing, type style, color, radius, shadow, motion lives in a typed TypeScript export. Audit at end of v1: one hardcoded hex across ~35 files. Dark-mode swap will be one line.

📐

Math shared across platforms

Income, priorities, savings, the 50/25/15/10 split, and the safe-to-spend formula live in @budgetplanner/core. Consumed by the mobile app today. Could ship to web tomorrow.

🛡️

Defensive persistence

The model already has one schema bump in it. Corrupt JSON, missing fields, or legacy shapes never throw. The app cannot crash on a storage edge case.

📊

One chart, on purpose

The brief allowed "no chart heavier than a thin progress arc." One mini arc on CategoryDetail, encoding two things at once — spend ratio and today's position in the month.

05The pivot

Three weeks in, after living with the app on a real device, the founder said:

I want to add a bit of color. Can we do something more like Coinbase?

This was a real test. The brief was explicit — "Monochrome surface. Category accent colors appear only on the category bar fills" — and we'd held the line for the entire build. Two options: push back, or absorb the feedback and adjust.

We absorbed it. In a 4-hour phase, the visual identity shifted:

SurfaceBeforeAfter
Primary buttonsInkIndigo
FAB ("+")InkIndigo
Active tab icon + labelInkIndigo
Section linksGray secondaryIndigo
Filter chips (selected)Ink-filled pillLight-indigo pill
Type systemSystem fontPoppins — full ladder
App icon + splashDefaultCustom logo
Category accentsUnchanged — they're functional, not brand

The lesson: Calm was the right starting point. Building with that constraint kept us from drowning the product in personality during initial development. Adding brand color after the structure was solid was a 4-hour change. Trying to design with brand color from day one would have produced a busier, more conventional fintech app.

06What we'd carry into the next project

The grill phase is the cheapest hour of any project.

Resolving 8 foundational decisions before any design work saved weeks downstream.

Tokens in code beat tokens in Figma.

When a designer renames a token and it propagates to the build, the "design vs. dev sync" problem disappears.

Naming is a design surface.

"Transaction" not "expense." "Close out" not "wrap up." "Adjust plan" not "edit budget." These decisions are load-bearing.

The brief should be a contract you're willing to break.

We held the monochrome line for the full build, then broke it deliberately when feedback said to. A brief that's never revisited is one that's secretly being ignored.

Restraint is the differentiator.

Two notifications, ever. No streaks. No gamification. No social. Almost every product instinct in personal finance pushes toward more. Less is what makes this readable.
